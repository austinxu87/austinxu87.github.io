About Me

I'm a PhD student at the School of Electrical and Computer Engineering and the Center for Machine Learning at Georgia Tech. I am fortunate to be advised by Mark Davenport and to be a ML@GT Fellow. I graduated with a BSE in Electrical Engineering from the University of Michigan in May 2019.

For summer 2023, I will be joining Duolingo Research as an AI Research intern.

From summer and fall 2022, I was a part-time Applied Scientist Intern at Amazon, where I worked with Arjun Seshadri, Mariya Vasileva, and Achal Dave on synthetic dataset generation with GANs.

My research is focuses on studying human data elicitation from both an empirical and theoretical perspective. Specifically, I am interested in how humans should be queried to provide feedback and when we can avoid asking for human feedback. As a result, my past work has focused on studying

  • Learning from simple relational queries, such as paired comparisons. For example, within the context of preference learning, can we learn what a human prefers along with how they measure preference?
  • New ways of querying humans with mathematically rigorous guarantees. This pursuit leverages tools from the rich field of high-dimensional statistics to develop theoretical guarantees, such as a bound on sample complexity, for learning from human feedback
  • Scenarios where we can bypass human interaction altogether. For example, within the context of synthetic dataset generation, can we produce complex labels (e.g., pixel-level semantic segmentation masks) without the need to query humans for additional labels?

In my free time, I enjoy cooking (and eating), reading, running, and watching basketball (NBA and college).
